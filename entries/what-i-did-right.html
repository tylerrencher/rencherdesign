What I got right building my own home.

Two years of daily feedback from nine full-time occupants. Here are the decisions I'd make again without hesitation — and why they worked. The reasoning matters more than the list, because reasoning is what transfers to a different project on a different piece of land for a different family.

Designing your own home is a strange experience. You make hundreds of decisions across months of planning, you build the thing, and then you live with those decisions every day — including the ones you can't undo. It's one of the most honest forms of feedback a designer can receive, and I've paid attention to it.

This entry is about the decisions that held up. Not in theory — in practice, after two years and nine full-time occupants. Some felt obvious at the time. Others I had to fight for. All of them are things I'd do again without hesitation, and most are things I now recommend to clients whenever the project allows it.

Infrastructure: the decisions that live inside finished walls

The infrastructure decisions I'm most grateful for are the ones I made early — conduit, wire, and networking runs that are now inside finished walls and would cost real money to add or change today. These decisions have a specific characteristic: they cost almost nothing to make correctly during construction, and they're essentially impossible to undo cheaply after the fact.

Multiple Ethernet runs to every room that matters

I pulled multiple Ethernet drops to the rooms where connectivity would matter — the office, the living areas, the kids' work spaces. This felt like mild over-engineering at the time. We've changed ISPs. We've changed how we route traffic. We've added equipment I didn't anticipate. Every time, the wired infrastructure was already there. If you're deciding whether to run one Ethernet drop per room or three, run three. The marginal cost is wire and an hour of labor. The marginal benefit compounds for the life of the house.

Antenna wire to the roof at multiple locations

I pulled coax to several roof penetration points during construction. We've used those runs for weather equipment, communication antenna, and other roof-mounted gear without ever running a wire through a finished wall. This is the kind of decision that only feels unnecessary until the day you need it — at which point you're either glad you did it or paying a premium to add it.

Conduit for solar PV wire and pre-wiring for rapid shutdown

Every solar wire run inside the building goes through conduit. Any wire can be pulled and replaced without opening a finished surface. The solar industry changes fast — inverter technology, monitoring systems, code requirements — and having a conduit infrastructure means I can modify the electrical side of the system without retrofitting anything on the exterior or cutting into walls. I also pre-wired for an emergency rapid shutdown switch, which is now required by code in many jurisdictions. Roughing it in before it was mandatory cost almost nothing. Adding it after the fact would have been a significant job.

Branch circuit level power monitoring and logging

I monitor power consumption at the individual circuit level and log it continuously. When the solar system isn't performing as expected, I can identify which loads are consuming more than anticipated. When something in the house is pulling power unexpectedly, I know immediately rather than at the end of the month. For anyone operating a home with solar and battery storage, circuit-level monitoring isn't a luxury — it's the instrument panel for the system you're responsible for.

Kitchen and pantry

The kitchen and pantry are where I put the most thought into daily workflow, and it shows. These are the decisions I feel best about in the whole house — and the ones that generate the most comments from visitors.

Drawers as primary cabinetry

I specified drawers as the primary storage format in the lower kitchen cabinets rather than doors with shelves behind them. Every item in a drawer is visible and accessible when you open it — you don't have to crouch, reach to the back, or pull things out to find what's underneath. Pots, pans, baking equipment, utensils — all immediately accessible. The cost difference over standard cabinets is real but modest. The daily quality-of-life difference is significant. I won't design a kitchen without this default again.

Planning exactly what goes in every drawer before the order was placed

Before finalizing the cabinet order, I made a list of every item that needed to be stored in the kitchen and assigned it a specific location. This forced the layout to be designed around actual use rather than generic storage. There are no drawers that were sized for a general purpose that turned out to be the wrong size for everything. Everything has a home and everything is where you expect it to be. Storage planning sounds tedious in the design phase. It's among the most valuable hours you can spend there.

Small appliance area in the pantry

I designated a specific zone in the pantry for small appliances — outlets, dedicated counter space, enough clearance for a stand mixer, a toaster, and equipment that gets used regularly but doesn't need to live on the main counter. This keeps the kitchen counter clear without requiring anyone to hunt through cabinets. The pantry outlet is on a dedicated circuit so multiple appliances can run simultaneously. Most kitchen layouts don't include this. It should be standard.

Two dishwashers and two ranges

With nine people in the house, a single dishwasher creates a permanent backlog. Two running simultaneously handle the load a large family generates without requiring hand-washing or queue management. Same logic applies to two ranges — two people can cook simultaneously without interfering with each other, and the redundancy is practical when one needs service. For large families, appliance redundancy is not a luxury — it's a design requirement that most designers don't raise with clients.

Mechanical systems

Heat pumps

I chose heat pumps over a conventional gas or propane system — a meaningful decision in an off-grid context where every kilowatt-hour has to be produced by solar and stored in batteries. Heat pumps move heat rather than generate it: for every unit of electrical energy they consume, they deliver two to four units of heat depending on outdoor temperature. The system has performed well, including in the colder temperatures this climate produces. For anyone building a high-efficiency home — particularly off-grid — heat pumps deserve serious consideration. The efficiency ratio changes the sizing and cost calculation for the entire electrical system.

Hybrid heat pump water heater

A hybrid heat pump water heater extracts heat from surrounding air rather than generating it from resistance elements — roughly three times as efficient as a conventional electric water heater in heat pump mode. It has been excellent. One planning note: it needs to be placed in a space with enough air volume and ambient warmth to operate effectively. That placement decision needs to happen during mechanical design, not after the space is already constrained by other equipment.

Multiple HVAC zones

Rather than conditioning the entire house as a single zone, I designed independent zones for different areas. The bedroom wing can be cooler at night without overcooling the rest of the house. The office can be conditioned during work hours without conditioning spaces that aren't occupied. For a large home with varied occupancy patterns throughout the day, zoning provides real efficiency and real comfort benefits — and it means that when one zone has a problem, the rest of the house isn't affected.

Spacing between garage bays

I gave the garage bays more lateral space between vehicles than the standard layout calls for. The difference is the ability to open a door fully without touching the vehicle next to you. This feels minor until you're loading car seats, carrying groceries, or managing gear with young children — at which point the extra eighteen inches is the difference between a frustrating space and a functional one. Standard garage dimensions are optimized for cars, not for the humans who use them with cars.

Details that affect daily life

Nightlight outlet covers

I installed outlet covers with built-in LED nightlights throughout the house. In a house with young children, not needing overhead lights for middle-of-the-night movement is a genuine quality-of-life difference. These cost almost nothing to install during rough-in and require no maintenance. The details that affect daily life deserve as much attention as the systems that make the news.

Kids' cubby under the stairs

I built dedicated cubbies under the stairs — one per child, sized for backpacks, shoes, and small gear. The space stays organized because every item has an assigned location and every person has assigned ownership. Under-stair space is easy to waste as a generic closet. Turning it into something specific and assigned makes it genuinely useful.

Nursery in the primary suite

I included a small nursery room adjacent to the primary bedroom with a connecting interior door. This only pays off in a specific season of life — but when it pays off, it pays off enormously. Having a newborn in an adjacent room rather than down the hall matters at 3 AM in ways that are hard to overstate. Two of my children were born in this house. The nursery was used for both. If there is any possibility you will have young children during the years you live in the home you're building, this is worth serious consideration.

Lighting quality throughout the house

I spent significant time on the lighting plan — fixture placement, color temperature, layering of ambient and task lighting, dimmer control. The result is a house that is genuinely pleasant to be in at night. Good residential lighting is surprisingly rare. Getting it right requires thinking about it as a design problem: which zones need what level of light, what color temperature suits each use, where task lighting needs to be independent of ambient. Lighting is worth the attention during design, when it costs nothing to change. After rough-in, it costs real money.
