Living off-grid with a family of nine: what we gave up, and what we didn't.

The most common assumption about off-grid homes is that you sacrifice comfort for independence. My family runs a dishwasher, washer and dryer, full kitchen appliances, and a forced-air heating system — all from solar and battery storage. Here's how that's possible, and what it actually took to design it.

Before we built our home, my wife and I visited every off-grid home we could find that was open to visitors. We wanted to see what this actually looked like at scale, in practice, with real families. What we found was consistent and discouraging: every home was either making serious compromises to daily comfort, or it was running primarily on propane — a fossil fuel with its own supply chain, its own costs, and its own dependence on something outside the property.

The compromises varied. Some homes were always slightly cold. Some had dim lighting because every watt counted. Some required the family to monitor power levels constantly and adjust behavior accordingly — running the dishwasher only on sunny days, deferring laundry during cloudy stretches, rationing hot water. That is a different way of living than what I wanted for my family. Not worse, necessarily — some people find it meaningful to live that closely with their energy system. But it wasn't what I was trying to build.

I wanted to build a home where nine people live the way nine people want to live. The dishwasher runs when it's full. The washer runs when there's laundry, which in a household of nine is roughly always. The house is warm in winter without anyone tending to it. And none of that requires us to think about our power system on a normal day. We have been doing this since 2023. This entry is what I learned.

What actually made it possible

The answer is not a bigger solar array. That's the instinctive response — if the house needs more power, add more panels — and it's the wrong starting point. The right starting point is the building envelope.

The biggest single electrical load in our home in winter is the HVAC system. In a standard construction home of our size in this climate, heating alone would draw somewhere between 3,000 and 6,000 kWh in a typical winter month. That load, run from solar and battery storage, would require an electrical system roughly three to four times the size of what we actually have — and it would make the system less reliable, not more, because the variance between a good solar month and a bad one becomes more consequential when the total load is higher.

Our home was designed from the beginning to minimize that heating load. The air sealing tested at 1.03 ACH50. The walls are R-25 with continuous exterior foam. The ceiling is R-60. The windows have a solar heat gain coating that makes them function as passive heaters on sunny days — on a clear day above 20°F, the heat system doesn't run at all. The result is that our total winter heating draw is a fraction of what it would be in a standard home. That reduction is what makes the electrical system feasible.

"The answer to making off-grid work isn't a bigger solar array. It's a smaller load — which means a better-built house."

This is the principle that most off-grid system designers miss, because most of them are not home designers. They size systems for the house as it is, not for the house as it could be. A high-performance building envelope is the most cost-effective component in an off-grid electrical system, because every unit of energy the house doesn't need is a unit that doesn't have to be generated, stored, and converted. The savings cascade through the entire system design.

What we actually run

For anyone trying to calibrate what off-grid at this scale looks like, here is what our household runs routinely, entirely on solar and battery storage:

Two dishwashers — in a family of nine, a single dishwasher runs constantly; two means we don't manage a queue. Two full-size ranges because my wife teaches cooking and we feed nine people at every meal. A washer and dryer. A hybrid heat pump water heater providing hot water for nine people's showers, dishes, and laundry. Two refrigerators and three chest freezers, because feeding nine people requires the storage capacity to buy and preserve food in quantity. A forced-air heat pump system for the whole house — not a wood stove, not propane, not resistance heat. A home network and server room, computers and monitors, and all the small appliances and electronics a large household generates.

We also run a central vacuum, a water treatment system, a VFD-controlled well pump, and lighting for 5,000 square feet. None of this is rationed on a normal day. None of it is scheduled around solar production. The system handles the load.

The well pump surprise: Our single biggest electrical load over the course of a winter is not the HVAC — it's the well pump. Without a storage tank to buffer demand, the pump cycles on every time house pressure drops, drawing 2.45 MWh over our first full winter. Both heat pump systems combined drew 2.24 MWh. If you're designing an off-grid home with a well, a pump-to-storage-tank strategy rather than direct pressure maintenance can meaningfully reduce this load.

What we actually gave up

Honesty requires this section, and I want to be specific rather than vague.

We think about power in January

On a normal day in spring, summer, or fall, we think about our power system approximately as much as grid-connected households think about their utility — which is to say, not at all. In January, during an extended cloudy stretch with high heating demand, we are aware of the state of the batteries. We make small adjustments: running the dishwashers during the day rather than overnight, deferring non-urgent laundry by a day or two, watching the overnight low and considering whether tomorrow's heating demand warrants running the generator tonight to top up the bank.

This is not the constant monitoring and rationing I observed in the homes we visited before building. But it is not zero. About six to eight weeks of the year — the heart of winter — require some active awareness of the system. The rest of the year, we don't think about it.

The generator exists and we use it

I want to be direct about this because many people planning off-grid homes underestimate it. Our generator is a designed part of the system. It is not a sign that the system is undersized or failing. It is there because sizing the solar array and battery bank to handle 100% of the worst-case winter load without any generator backup would require a system three times the size and cost, with no meaningful improvement in daily life. The generator runs during bad winter stretches and that is exactly what it was designed to do.

Managing the generator is the most significant operational difference between our household and a grid-connected one. Ours is diesel, which requires preheating before startup and can't be automated with modern inverters the way propane can. Running it involves a multi-step manual process. I'd recommend propane to clients for this reason — propane generators integrate cleanly with modern inverters and can be fully automated. But even with a propane generator and full automation, an off-grid home in a serious winter climate requires accepting that generator runtime is part of the system, not a failure of it.

The condensing dryer

We use a condensing dryer rather than a vented one. A condensing dryer doesn't exhaust to the exterior — it condenses moisture from the clothes into a reservoir that gets emptied periodically. The reason we have one is that I didn't rough in a dryer duct to the exterior during construction, and adding one now would be expensive. This was a mistake I've documented elsewhere.

Condensing dryers work, but they're slower than vented dryers, require occasional maintenance, and are less effective on heavy items. In a household doing as much laundry as ours, this is a genuine daily friction point. A vented dryer in a high-performance home requires a well-sealed, dampered exterior penetration — but it's completely achievable, and I'd do it that way if I were starting over.

Snow and solar panels

Our roof is a 4:12 pitch — shallow enough that snow accumulates on the panels rather than sliding off. In a heavy snow year, clearing them is a regular winter task that can be mildly hazardous. A steeper roof or a ground-mounted array would eliminate this entirely. I've thought about it more than once at the top of a ladder in February.

What we don't think about at all

This part surprises people who ask about off-grid living, because the assumption is that the list of things given up is long. It isn't.

We have never had a power outage. The grid fails somewhere in our county several times a year — weather events, equipment failures, scheduled maintenance. We are not on the grid. We don't notice. When neighbors lose power in a winter storm, we are fully operational.

We have never received a utility bill that surprised us. We have no utility bills. The cost of our power system was paid at construction. The ongoing cost is generator fuel during bad winter stretches and routine equipment maintenance. That cost is a fraction of what a grid-connected home our size would pay annually in utilities.

We have never had a furnace fail during a cold snap. The heat pump systems are reliable, and critically, our house holds temperature for an extended period even without active heating — because the envelope is tight enough that heat loss is slow. If the system failed entirely during a cold stretch, we would have hours, not minutes, before the house became uncomfortably cold.

We have never wondered whether utilities will eventually reach our land. We bought a property miles from the nearest power line. That property was affordable partly because most buyers would have faced significant extension costs. We did not. The distance from the grid was an advantage, not a limitation.

The decisions that made the difference

If I were advising a family planning an off-grid home today, these are the decisions I would emphasize — the ones that separate our experience from the compromised homes we visited before we built.

Start with the envelope, not the system. The building envelope determines the load. The load determines the system size. A leaky, poorly insulated home requires a large, expensive, unreliable off-grid system. A tight, well-insulated home requires a small, affordable, reliable one. This sequence is not optional.

Heat pumps, not propane. A heat pump delivers two to four units of heat for every unit of electrical energy consumed. A propane furnace delivers less than one — and introduces a supply chain dependency. Heat pumps are harder to size correctly for off-grid applications because efficiency drops in very cold weather, but sized correctly for the actual load of a tight building, they are the right choice for a modern all-electric off-grid home.

Size the system for January, not the annual average. The month your system must survive is the month with the shortest days, least solar production, and highest heating demand. A system sized from annual production numbers will fail in January. A system sized from January performance will work all year. I've written about this at length in the solar sizing entry.

Design the generator in from the start. An off-grid home in a real winter climate needs a generator. Accepting that early and designing around it — choosing propane for automation, sizing it to run efficiently under the intended load, integrating it cleanly with the inverter system — produces a much better result than treating the generator as a reluctant backup that signals failure.

Monitor at the circuit level. Branch circuit monitoring is among the best investments in an off-grid home. Knowing exactly where power is going — not just total consumption, but which loads are drawing how much — is how you identify surprises early and make informed decisions during high-demand periods. It is also how you discover that your well pump is your biggest winter load.

Whether it's worth it

That question has two honest answers depending on what you're asking.

Financially, for our specific situation — rural land miles from utility infrastructure, a large home with high baseline energy needs — yes, clearly. The cost of extending power to our property would have been substantial. The ongoing utility cost for a home our size would be significant. We paid more upfront for the high-performance building and the electrical system, and we spend less on an ongoing basis than grid-connected households of comparable size. The math works over any reasonable time horizon.

As a way to live, that's more personal. We value the independence. We value knowing what powers our home and where it comes from. We value the absence of utility bills, power outages, and dependence on infrastructure we don't control. We accept a few weeks per year of active system management as the price of that independence and don't find it burdensome. Others might.

What I'd push back on is the premise that off-grid living requires accepting a lower standard of comfort. It doesn't — not if the home is designed correctly. The families I visited before we built had lower comfort because they had homes that were expensive to heat and poorly suited to off-grid systems. Ours is comfortable because we designed the building and the system together, with performance as the starting constraint rather than an afterthought. That is what the design process I offer produces — not just drawings, but a home that actually works the way the family needs it to.
