Your entry title goes here — write it to promise something specific

This is the lede — the opening hook that earns the click. Two or three sentences. Be specific about what the reader will learn or understand differently by the end. Write this last, after the entry is finished.

This is the opening paragraph of your entry. It continues the promise made in the lede and draws the reader into the substance of the piece. Don't restate the title — move forward immediately into the content. A good opening paragraph earns the reader's investment in the next one.

This is a second paragraph. Each paragraph should make one clear point and lead naturally to the next. Write the way you talk — first person, direct, technically credible. The voice that runs through the rest of the site should run through the journal too.

"This is a pull quote — a single sentence that earns being set apart. Use it for the line that best captures the entry's central argument."

This paragraph continues after the pull quote. The pull quote isn't a break from the argument — it's a highlight of it. Keep writing as though it's still part of the same flow.

This is a major section heading

After a section heading, continue directly into the content of that section. Don't introduce it — the heading already did that. The first paragraph after a heading should be the most important point of that section, not a preamble to it.

Practical note: This is a callout box. Use it to break out a specific technical detail, rule of thumb, or practical implication that would interrupt the flow if left in the prose. Keep callouts short — two or three sentences at most.

Prose continues after the callout. The callout is a sidebar, not a replacement for the surrounding argument. The paragraphs before and after it should connect without depending on it.

This paragraph wraps around the floated photo on the right. Use floated photos when the image is secondary to the text — an illustration rather than the main point. The clearfix div below resets the float before the next element.

A second paragraph can also wrap around the same floated image if the photo is tall enough. If the text is shorter than the image, the next section element will start beside the photo rather than below it — use the clearfix to prevent this.

This is a sub-heading within a section

Sub-headings are for breaking a long section into named parts. Use them when a section covers two or three distinct sub-points that each deserve their own label. Don't use them just to add visual variety — every heading should mean something.

This paragraph follows the side-by-side photos. The photo pair works best for before/after comparisons, two angles of the same subject, or two related but distinct details. If both photos show the same thing, use a single full-width image instead.

Another major section

A horizontal rule signals a significant shift — a new argument, a time jump, or a change of subject within the same entry. Use it sparingly. If you find yourself using it between every section, the entry probably needs to be restructured rather than divided.

The three-photo row works best for a sequence, a set of related details, or multiple angles of one subject. All three images are cropped to the same 4:3 aspect ratio automatically. Use photos with similar lighting and framing for the strongest result.

This is the closing paragraph of the entry. End with something worth remembering — the takeaway, the implication, or the question the entry leaves the reader with. Don't summarize what you just wrote. End with a forward motion, not a recap.
